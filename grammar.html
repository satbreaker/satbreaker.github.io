Beat the SAT: Grammar and Sentence Completion

Good news and bad news. Good news: The SAT tests 20 different grammar rules, so if you can understand them, you shouldn't get more than a question or two wrong on the entire writing portion of the SAT. Bad news: There's 20 rules, and to do well, you have to have an solid understanding of most of them. The rules are listed below, so go over a block/section per day, and by the test date, you'll have them all under your belt, ready to answer every question correctly!

Note: This method does not work for everyone. If this doesn't work for you, keep practicing and find out what does!

Extra Tips

COMMON WORD PAIRS ON SAT: Either/Or, Neither/Nor, Not Only/But Also, Both/And, Between/AND *IT’S NOT OR.* As/as, (more/less)/than, (so/such)/that, from/to, just as/so.
Things to Remember: 1) Who refers to people 2) That/Which refer to things 3) When refers to time 4) Where refers to places ONLY (Often used as a faulty comparison when talking about things)
Tips for Guessing: Shorter IS better, -ing words are often used INCORRECTLY, PASSIVE VOICE is bad, LOOK TO MAINTAIN MEANING.

Section One

  • Pronouns - SUBJECT= He She We They I ; OBJECT= Him Her Us Them Me **After prepositional phrase, always use OBJECT PRONOUN.
  • Adjectives and Adverbs - Switched with each other, if underlined, make sure it’s used correctly.
  • Parallelism - Make sure parallelism is constant throughout the sentence.
  • Prepositions and Idioms - Check by ear, If it doesn’t sound right, it PROBABLY ISN’T
  • “Familiar WITH” not “FAMILIAR IN” (Familiar with the rules)
  • Comparisons - Compare things with things, people with people. CHECK COMPARISONS CAREFULLY.

Section Two

  • Subject Verb Agreement - Cross off parenthethical/comma clause to check if it makes sense.
  • Subject Verb Agreement - There is/has = singular; there are/have = plural ; Neither (sing. Noun) nor (singular noun)= singular. LOOK at last noun when deciding in combined subjects with Or or Nor; Gerunds(when used as noun, ‘running’), Each, every, collective nouns, what, whatever are considered singular
  • Verb Tense - Keep verbs all in same tense.
  • Present Perfect v. Simple Past (has drunk vs. Drank) - Use Simple Past in any sentence with reference to a time, Present Perfect is just a non specified time period.
  • Past Participle v. Simple Past - Simple past will always be incorrectly paired with “to have” (to have grew should be to have grown)

Section Three

  • Sentence Fragments - Using Gerund instead of verb b- using NO verb at all
  • Commas and SemiColons - For And Nor But Or Yet So (FANBOYS); When using a FANBOY to join two indep. Clauses, use COMMA; When connecting indep. And dep. Clause with FANBOY, NO COMMA.
  • Comma Splice - Means Comma but NO FANBOY or PREP. PHRASE ALWAYS INCORRECT
  • Semicolons - Never use before fanboy, always use a comma. ALWAYS use before HOWEVER, NEVERTHELESS, MOREOVER, CONSEQUENTLY when those words start a new clause.
  • Dangling Modifiers - FIND OUT WHAT THE PHRASE IS MODIFYING, place that word as close as possible to phrase and then CHECK.
  • Wordiness - Cross Out NONESSENTIAL Phrases to check S/V Agreement, Modification, etc.
